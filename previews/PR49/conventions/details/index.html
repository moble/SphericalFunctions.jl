Details

This page carefully works through all the conventions used in this package, starting from first principles to motivate the choices and ensure that each step is on firm footing. The previous page collects the results in a more concise form.

Note that we will use Euler angles and spherical coordinates here, but they are not used internally in this package вҖ” though conversion functions are available. It is almost always a bad idea to use Euler angles in computing; quaternions are clearly the preferred representation for numerous reasons. However, Euler angles are important for (a) comparing to other sources, and (b) performing analytic integrations. These are the only two uses we will make of Euler angles.

Three-dimensional space

The space we are working in is naturally three-dimensional Euclidean space, so we start with a right-handed Cartesian coordinate system $(x, y, z)$. These also give us the unit basis vectors $(рқҗұ, рқҗІ, рқҗі)$. Note that these basis vectors are assumed to have unit norm, but we omit the hats just to keep the notation simple. Any vector in this space can be written as

\[рқҗҜ = v_x рқҗұ + v_y рқҗІ + v_z рқҗі,\]

in which case the Euclidean norm is given by

\[\| рқҗҜ \| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2 + v_z^2}.\]

Equivalently, we can write the components of the Euclidean metric as

\[g_{ij} = \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right)_{ij}.\]

Note that, because the points of the space are in one-to-one correspondence with the vectors, we will frequently use a vector to label a point in space.

We will be working on the sphere, so it will be very convenient to use spherical coordinates $(r, Оё, П•)$. We choose the standard "physics" conventions for these, in which we relate to the Cartesian coordinates by

\[\begin{aligned} r &= \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2} &&\in [0, \infty), \\ Оё &= \arccos\left(\frac{z}{r}\right) &&\in [0, ПҖ], \\ П• &= \arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) &&\in [0, 2ПҖ), \end{aligned}\]

where we assume the $\arctan$ in the expression for $П•$ is really the two-argument form that gives the correct quadrant. The inverse transformation is given by

\[\begin{aligned} x &= r \sin Оё \cos П•, \\ y &= r \sin Оё \sin П•, \\ z &= r \cos Оё. \end{aligned}\]

We can use this to find the components of the metric in spherical coordinates:

\[g_{i'j'} = \sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial x^i}{\partial x^{i'}} \frac{\partial x^j}{\partial x^{j'}} g_{ij} = \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & r^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & r^2 \sin^2Оё \end{array} \right)_{i'j'}.\]

The unit coordinate vectors in spherical coordinates are then

\[\begin{aligned} рқҗ« &= \sin Оё \cos П• рқҗұ + \sin Оё \sin П• рқҗІ + \cos Оё рқҗі, \\ \boldsymbol{Оё} &= \cos Оё \cos П• рқҗұ + \cos Оё \sin П• рқҗІ - \sin Оё рқҗі, \\ \boldsymbol{П•} &= -\sin П• рқҗұ + \cos П• рқҗІ, \end{aligned}\]

where, again, we omit the hats on the unit vectors to keep the notation simple. Conversely, we can express the Cartesian basis vectors in terms of the spherical basis vectors as

\[\begin{aligned} рқҗұ &= \sin Оё \cos П• рқҗ« + \cos Оё \cos П• \boldsymbol{Оё} - \sin П• \boldsymbol{П•}, \\ рқҗІ &= \sin Оё \sin П• рқҗ« + \cos Оё \sin П• \boldsymbol{Оё} + \cos П• \boldsymbol{П•}, \\ рқҗі &= \cos Оё рқҗ« - \sin Оё \boldsymbol{Оё}. \end{aligned}\]

One seemingly obvious вҖ” but extremely important вҖ” fact is that the unit basis frame $(рқҗұ, рқҗІ, рқҗі)$ can be rotated onto $(\boldsymbol{Оё}, \boldsymbol{П•}, рқҗ«)$ by first rotating through the "polar" angle $Оё$ about the $рқҗІ$ axis, and then through the "azimuthal" angle $П•$ about the $рқҗі$ axis. This becomes important when we consider spin-weighted functions.

Integration in Cartesian coordinates is, of course, trivial as

\[\int_{\mathbb{R}^3} f\, d^3рқҗ« = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f\, dx\, dy\, dz.\]

In spherical coordinates, the integrand involves the square-root of the determinant of the metric, so we have

\[\int_{\mathbb{R}^3} f\, d^3рқҗ« = \int_0^\infty \int_0^ПҖ \int_0^{2ПҖ} f\, r^2 \sin Оё\, dr\, dОё\, dП•.\]

Restricting to the unit sphere, we obtain the usual surface element

\[\int_{рқ•ҠВІ} f\, d^2\Omega = \int_0^ПҖ \int_0^{2ПҖ} f\, \sin Оё\, dОё\, dП•.\]

Note that $\int_{рқ•ҠВІ} d^2\Omega = 4ПҖ$.

Four-dimensional space: Quaternions and rotations

Geometric algebra

Given the basis vectors $(рқҗұ, рқҗІ, рқҗі)$ and the Euclidean norm, we can define the geometric algebra. The key feature is the geometric product, which we could define for any pair of vectors as $рқҗҜ$ and $рқҗ°$ as

\[рқҗҜ рқҗ° = рқҗҜ вӢ… рқҗ° + рқҗҜ вҲ§ рқҗ°,\]

where the dot product is the usual scalar product and the wedge product is the antisymmetric part of the tensor product вҖ”В acting just like the standard exterior product from the algebra of differential forms. The geometric product is linear, associative, distributive, and has the property that

\[рқҗҜрқҗҜ = \| рқҗҜ \|^2.\]

The most useful properties of the geometric product are that parallel vectors commute with each other, while orthogonal vectors anticommute. Since the geometric product is linear, the product of any two vectors can be decomposed into parallel and orthogonal parts.

The basis for this entire space is then the set

\[\begin{gather} рқҹҸ, \\ рқҗұ, рқҗІ, рқҗі,\\ рқҗұрқҗІ, рқҗұрқҗі, рқҗІрқҗі, \\ рқҗұрқҗІрқҗі. \end{gather}\]

The standard presentation of quaternions (including the confused historical development) uses different symbols for these last four basis elements:

\[\begin{gather} рқҗў = рқҗірқҗІ = -рқҗІрқҗі, \\ рқҗЈ = рқҗұрқҗі = -рқҗірқҗұ, \\ рқҗӨ = рқҗІрқҗұ = -рқҗұрқҗІ, \\ рқҗҲ = рқҗұрқҗІрқҗі. \end{gather}\]

Note that each of these squares to -1. For example, recalling that orthogonal vectors anticommute, the product is associative, and the product of a vector with itself is just its squared norm, we have

\[рқҗұрқҗІрқҗұрқҗІ = -рқҗұрқҗІрқҗІрқҗұ = -рқҗұ(рқҗІрқҗІ)рқҗұ = -рқҗұрқҗұ = -1.\]

Any of these could act like the unit imaginary; $рқҗұрқҗІ$ is probably the canonical choice.

$рқҗҲ$ is sometimes called the pseudoscalar. Its inverse is $рқҗҲ^{-1} = рқҗірқҗІрқҗұ = -рқҗұрқҗІрқҗі$, which can also serve as something very much like the Hodge star operator,[1] mapping elements to their "dual" elements. In particular, we have

\[\begin{aligned} рқҗў &= рқҗҲ^{-1}рқҗұ, \\ рқҗЈ &= рқҗҲ^{-1}рқҗІ, \\ рқҗӨ &= рқҗҲ^{-1}рқҗі. \end{aligned}\]

We will see that $рқҗў$ generates right-handed rotations in the positive sense about $рқҗұ$, $рқҗЈ$ about $рқҗІ$, and $рқҗӨ$ about $рқҗі$. Moreover, this mapping between $(рқҗұ, рқҗІ, рқҗі)$ and $(рқҗў, рқҗЈ, рқҗӨ)$ is a vector-space isomorphism. In fact, the reader who is not familiar with geometric algebra but is familiar with quaternions may be able to read an expression like $рқҗЈ рқҗұ рқҗЈвҒ»В№$ as if it is just an abuse of notation, and mentally replace $рқҗұ$ with $рқҗў$ to read those symbols as a valid quaternion expression; both viewpoints are equally correct by the isomorphism.

Quaternions and Euler angles

Note that there are different conventions for the signs of the $(рқҗў, рқҗЈ, рқҗӨ)$ basis. Everyone agrees that $рқҗўВІ = рқҗЈВІ = рқҗӨВІ = -1$, but we could easily flip the sign of any basis element, and these would still be satisfied. The identifications we chose above are made to ensure that $рқҗў$ generates rotations about $рқҗұ$, and so on, but even that depends on how we define quaternions as acting on vectors (to be discussed below). A different choice of the latter would result in all flipping the sign of all three basis elements, which is a convention that is commonly used вҖ” though almost exclusively in aerospace. The key expressions that eliminate ambiguity are the multiplications

\[\begin{aligned} рқҗў рқҗЈ &= рқҗӨ, \\ рқҗЈ рқҗӨ &= рқҗў, \\ рқҗӨ рқҗў &= рқҗЈ. \end{aligned}\]

We can also use these rules above to determine $рқҗўрқҗЈрқҗӨ = -рқҹҸ$. All four of these equations have flipped signs in other conventions. See Sommer et al. for a discussion of the different conventions.

We use coordinates $(W, X, Y, Z)$ on the space of quaternions, so that a quaternion would be written as

\[рқҗҗ = WрқҹҸ + Xрқҗў + YрқҗЈ + ZрқҗӨ,\]

though we usually omit the $рқҹҸ$. The space of all quaternions is thus four dimensional. The norm is just the standard Euclidean norm, so that the norm of a quaternion is

\[\| рқҗҗ \| = \sqrt{W^2 + X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2}.\]

An important operation is the conjugate, which is defined as

\[\overline{рқҗҗ} = W - Xрқҗў - YрқҗЈ - ZрқҗӨ.\]

Note that the squared norm can be written as the quaternion times its conjugate. Any nonzero quaternion has an inverse, which is just the conjugate divided by the squared norm:

\[рқҗҗ^{-1} = \frac{\overline{рқҗҗ}}{рқҗҗ\overline{рқҗҗ}} = \frac{\overline{рқҗҗ}}{\| рқҗҗ \|^2}.\]

The other important operation is exponentiation. Since a scalar commutes with any quaternion, including a nonzero scalar component in the quaternion will simply multiply the result by the exponential of that scalar component. Moreover, we will not have any use for such an exponential, so we assume that the argument to the exponential function is a "pure" quaternion вҖ” that is, one with zero scalar component. Moreover, we write it as a unit quaternion $рқҗ®$ times some real number $\sigma$. In particular, note that $рқҗ®^2 = -1$, so that it acts like the imaginary unit, which means we already know how to exponentiate it:

\[\exp(рқҗ®\, \sigma) = \cos\sigma + рқҗ®\, \sin\sigma.\]

Note that the inverse of the result can be obtained simply by negating the argument, as usual.

Much as with standard three-dimensional space, we could introduce a generalization of spherical coordinates, though we use a slight variant: extended Euler coordinates. We will see below how to interpret these as a series of rotations. For now, we simply state the relation:

\[\begin{aligned} R &= \sqrt{W^2 + X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2} &&\in [0, \infty), \\ Оұ &= \arctan\frac{Z}{W} + \arctan\frac{-X}{Y} &&\in [0, 2ПҖ), \\ ОІ &= 2\arccos\sqrt{\frac{W^2+Z^2}{W^2+X^2+Y^2+Z^2}} &&\in [0, ПҖ], \\ Оі &= \arctan\frac{Z}{W} - \arctan\frac{-X}{Y} &&\in [0, 4ПҖ), \end{aligned}\]

where we again assume the $\arctan$ in the expressions for $Оұ$ and $Оі$ is really the two-argument form that gives the correct quadrant, and if relevant, we use mod to limit the values on output. Note that here, $Оі$ ranges up to $4ПҖ$ rather than just $2ПҖ$, as in the standard Euler angles. This is because we are describing the space of quaternions, rather than just the space of rotations. If we restrict to quaternions with magnitude $R=1$, we have exactly the group of unit quaternions $\mathrm{Spin}(3)=\mathrm{SU}(2)$, which is a double cover of the rotation group $\mathrm{SO}(3)$. This extended range for $Оі$ is necessary to cover the entire space of quaternions; if we further restrict to $[0, 2ПҖ)$, we would only cover the space of rotations. This and the inclusion of $R$ identify precisely how this coordinate system extends the standard Euler angles.

Note that it would also be reasonable to limit $Оі$ to $2ПҖ$, while allowing $ОІ$ to range up to $2ПҖ$ to cover the entire space of quaternions. This is just somewhat more delicate to compute, and is simply not conventional. Also, using $Оі вҲҲ [0,4ПҖ)$ integrates nicely with our framework of a telescope with $Оі$ representing the rotation about its line of sight; a full $4ПҖ$ rotation is required for the polarizer to explore the full range of states of half-integer spin fields.

The inverse transformation is given by

\[\begin{aligned} W &= R\, \cos\frac{ОІ}{2} \cos\frac{Оұ+Оі}{2}, \\ X &= -R\, \sin\frac{ОІ}{2} \sin\frac{Оұ-Оі}{2}, \\ Y &= R\, \sin\frac{ОІ}{2} \cos\frac{Оұ-Оі}{2}, \\ Z &= R\, \cos\frac{ОІ}{2} \sin\frac{Оұ+Оі}{2}. \end{aligned}\]

As with the spherical coordinates, we can use this to find the components of the metric in our extended Euler coordinates:

\[g_{i'j'} = \sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial X^i}{\partial X^{i'}} \frac{\partial X^j}{\partial X^{j'}} g_{ij} = \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{R^2}{4} & 0 & \frac{R^2 \cos ОІ}{4} \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{R^2}{4} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{R^2 \cos ОІ}{4} & 0 & \frac{R^2}{4} \end{array} \right)_{i'j'}.\]

The unit basis vectors in extended Euler coordinates in terms of the unit basis vectors in quaternion coordinates are

\[\begin{aligned} рқҗ‘ &= \frac{1}{R} \left( \cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҹҸ - \sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗў + \sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗЈ + \cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҗӨ \right), \\ \boldsymbol{Оұ} &= \frac{R}{2} \left( -\cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҹҸ - \sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗў - \sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗЈ + \cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҗӨ \right), \\ \boldsymbol{ОІ} &= \frac{R}{2} \left( -\sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҹҸ - \cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗў + \cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗЈ - \sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҗӨ \right), \\ \boldsymbol{Оі} &= \frac{R}{2} \left( -\cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҹҸ + \sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗў - \sin \frac{ОІ}{2} \cos \frac{Оұ-Оі}{2} рқҗЈ - \cos \frac{ОІ}{2} \sin \frac{Оұ+Оі}{2} рқҗӨ \right). \end{aligned}\]

Again, integration involves a square-root of the determinant of the metric, which reduces to $R^3 \sin ОІ / 8$. The integral over the entire space of quaternions is then

\[\int_{\mathbb{R}^4} f\, d^4рқҗҗ = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty f\, dW\, dX\, dY\, dZ = \int_0^\infty \int_0^{2ПҖ} \int_0^{ПҖ} \int_0^{4ПҖ} f\, \frac{R^3}{8} \sin ОІ\, dR\, dОұ\, dОІ\, dОі.\]

Restricting to the unit sphere, we can simplify this to

\[\int_{\mathrm{Spin}(3)} f\, d^3\Omega = \int_0^{2ПҖ} \int_0^{ПҖ} \int_0^{4ПҖ} f\, \sin ОІ\, dОұ\, dОІ\, dОі,\]

where $\int_{\mathrm{Spin}(3)} d^3\Omega = 2ПҖ^2$. Finally, restricting to the space of rotations, we can further simplify this to

\[\int_{\mathrm{SO}(3)} f\, d^3\Omega = \int_0^{2ПҖ} \int_0^{ПҖ} \int_0^{2ПҖ} f\, \sin ОІ\, dОұ\, dОІ\, dОі,\]

where $\int_{\mathrm{SO}(3)} d^3\Omega = ПҖ^2$.

Rotations

We restrict to a unit quaternion $рқҗ‘$, for which $W^2 + X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2 = 1$. Given this constraint we can, without loss of generality, write the quaternion as

\[рқҗ‘ = \exp\left(\frac{\rho}{2} \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\right) = \cos\frac{\rho}{2} + \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ},\]

where $\rho$ is an angle of rotation and $\hat{рқ”Ҝ}$ is a unit "pure-vector" quaternion. We can multiply a vector $рқҗҜ$ as

\[рқҗ‘\, рқҗҜ\, рқҗ‘^{-1}.\]

Splitting $рқҗҜ = рқҗҜ_вҹӮ + рқҗҜ_вҲҘ$ into components perpendicular and parallel to $\hat{рқ”Ҝ}$, we see that $рқҗҜ_вҲҘ$ commutes with $рқҗ‘$ and $рқҗ‘^{-1}$, while $рқҗҜ_вҹӮ$ anticommutes with $\hat{рқ”Ҝ}$. To find the full rotation, we expand the product:

\[\begin{aligned} рқҗ‘\, рқҗҜ\, рқҗ‘^{-1} &= рқҗҜ_вҲҘ + \left(\cos\frac{\rho}{2} + \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\right) рқҗҜ_вҹӮ \left(\cos\frac{\rho}{2} - \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\right) \\ &= рқҗҜ_вҲҘ + \left(\cos\frac{\rho}{2}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ + \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ\right) \left(\cos\frac{\rho}{2} - \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\right) \\ &= рқҗҜ_вҲҘ + \cos^2\frac{\rho}{2}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ + \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \cos\frac{\rho}{2}\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ - \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \cos\frac{\rho}{2}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ \, \hat{рқ”Ҝ} - \sin^2\frac{\rho}{2}\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ} \\ &= рқҗҜ_вҲҘ + \cos^2\frac{\rho}{2}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ + \sin\frac{\rho}{2}\, \cos\frac{\rho}{2}\, [\hat{рқ”Ҝ}, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ] - \sin^2\frac{\rho}{2}\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ \\ &= рқҗҜ_вҲҘ + \cos\rho\, рқҗҜ_вҹӮ + \sin\rho\, \hat{рқ”Ҝ}\times рқҗҜ_вҹӮ \end{aligned}\]

The final expression shows that this is precisely what we expect when rotating $рқҗҜ$ through an angle $\rho$ (in a positive, right-handed sense) about the axis $\hat{рқ”Ҝ}$.

Note that the presence of two factors of $рқҗ‘$ in the expression for rotating a vector explains two things. First, it explains why the angle of rotation is twice the angle of the quaternion: one factor of $рқҗ‘$ either commutes and cancels or anti-commutes and combines with the the other factor. Second, it explains why the quaternion group is a double cover of the rotation group: negating $рқҗ‘$ results in the same rotation. Thus, for any rotation, there are two (precisely opposite) quaternions that represent it.

Euler angles and spherical coordinates

Now that we understand how rotations work, we can provide geometric intuition for the expressions given above for Euler angles. The Euler angles in our convention represent an initial rotation through $Оі$ about the $рқҗі$ axis, followed by a rotation through $ОІ$ about the $рқҗІ$ axis, and finally a rotation through $Оұ$ about the $рқҗі$ axis. Note that the axes are fixed, and not subject to any preceding rotations. More precisely, we can write the unit quaternion as

\[рқҗ‘ = \exp\left(\frac{Оұ}{2} рқҗӨ\right) \exp\left(\frac{ОІ}{2} рқҗЈ\right) \exp\left(\frac{Оі}{2} рқҗӨ\right).\]

One of the more important interpretations of a rotor is considering what it does to the basis triad $(рқҗұ, рқҗІ, рқҗі)$. In particular, the vector $рқҗі$ is rotated onto the point given by spherical coordinates $(Оё, П•) = (ОІ, Оұ)$, while $рқҗұ$ and $рқҗІ$ are rotated into the plane spanned by the unit basis vectors $\boldsymbol{Оё}$ and $\boldsymbol{П•}$ corresponding to that point. If $Оі = 0$ the rotation is precise, meaning that $рқҗұ$ is rotated onto $\boldsymbol{Оё}$ and $рқҗІ$ onto $\boldsymbol{П•}$; if $Оі вү  0$ then they are rotated within that plane by the angle $Оі$ about the $рқҗ«$ axis. Thus, we identify the spherical coordinates $(Оё, П•)$ with the Euler angles $(Оұ, ОІ, Оі) = (П•, Оё, 0)$.

Rotation and angular-momentum operators

Complex-valued functions

Starting with Cartesian coordinates and the Euclidean norm on $\mathbb{R}^3$, we have constructed the geometric algebra over that space, as well as the spaces $\mathrm{Spin}(3) = \mathrm{SU}(2)$ (topologically $рқ•ҠВі$), $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ (topologically $\mathbb{RP}^3$), and $рқ•ҠВІ$. We will be defining complex-valued functions on these spaces, and defining operators to construct and classify them. In particular, because we have constructed the spaces, they are naturally supplied with coordinates that are effectively inherited from the original Cartesian system. We will be using these coordinate systems to construct both the operators and functions. However, it is important to note that the coordinate systems may have singularities, which means that the spaces of coordinates may have different topologies than the spaces they represent. For example, Euler angles have topology $рқ•ҠВ№ \times I \times рқ•ҠВ№$ instead of the $рқ•ҠВі$ and $\mathbb{RP}^3$ topologies of the spaces they represent; spherical coordinates have topology $рқ•ҠВ№ \times I$ instead of $рқ•ҠВІ$.

Defining functions on the coordinate system of a space is subtly different from defining functions on the space itself. For example, spin-weighted functions are generally written as functions of ($рқ•ҠВІ$) spherical coordinates. However, they cannot be defined as functions on $рқ•ҠВІ$ itself; some notion of a reference tangent direction is needed at each point. The difference is that spherical coordinates supply a natural choice for the reference tangent direction: the unit vector in the $\boldsymbol{Оё}$ direction. This supplies just enough information to define the spin-weighted functions вҖ” though this ends up not being a useful form when more general transformations or deeper understanding are needed.

Because of this variety of spaces, we will need to use function composition in several ways; functions defined on one space can be "lifted" or "lowered" to another via maps between the spaces. In the diagram below, the function $F$ can be used to define the function $f$ via the mapping $m$ as $f = m \circ F$.

For example, $A$ could be the space of spherical coordinates, $B$ could be $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$, and $F$ could be a spin-weighted function. There are many maps from spherical coordinates into $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$; we expect that all such maps will be related by rotations from $\mathrm{SO}(3)$, and in some sense equivalent via some universality relation. However, for singular maps вҖ” such as coordinate singularities where multiple coordinate values correspond to a single "physical" point вҖ” we find exceptions to the universality. These compositions will be useful, in that we can define functions on the "largest" available space, and extend them to any space that maps into the first.

In principle, our functions should be defined on $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$ or even the quaternions in general, though in practice we will define them on the space of coordinates on those spaces. In any case, we will classify the functions by their behavior with respect to actions of $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$ on the argument to the function. Therefore, we need to consider the general behavior of functions under such actions.

Finite rotations

We work with functions $f: A \to \mathbb{C}$, where $A$ is either the group of unit quaternions, or the full algebra of quaternions. Any non-zero quaternion can be expressed as $e^рқ”Ө$ for some finite quaternion $рқ”Ө$, which is referred to as the "generator" of the action of $e^рқ”Ө$. This can act on a function $f$ by multiplying the argument by $e^рқ”Ө$. However, there is an ambiguity: we could multiply either on the left or the right:[2]

\[f\left(рқҗҗ\right) \mapsto f\left(e^рқ”Ө рқҗҗ\right) \qquad \text{or} \qquad f\left(рқҗҗ\right) \mapsto f\left(рқҗҗ e^рқ”Ө\right).\]

There is an additional ambiguity, in that this action rotates the argument of the function, whereas we will often prefer to think in terms of rotating the function itself. For example, our function may describe the measurement of some field in a particular coordinate system. Here, the argument $рқҗҗ$ describes a particular value of the coordinates, and $e^рқ”Ө$ changes the point under consideration. If, on the other hand $e^рқ”Ө$ describes how the field itself is rotated, then we can write the rotated field as a function $f'$ which is related to the original function $f$ by

\[f'\left(рқҗҗ\right) = f\left(e^{-рқ”Ө} рқҗҗ\right) \qquad \text{or} \qquad f'\left(рқҗҗ\right) = f\left(рқҗҗ e^{-рқ”Ө}\right).\]

Note that the exponent is negated, because the action of $e^рқ”Ө$ on the argument is the inverse of the action of $e^{-рқ”Ө}$ on the function. This is a general property of the action of a group on a space, and is a consequence of the group action being a homomorphism.

To validate the signs here, it may be helpful to work through a simple example involving the sphere $рқ•ҠВІ$. We define a function on spherical coordinates as

\[f(Оё, П•) = \sin Оё \sin П•.\]

Recall that we can map the spherical coordinates into the Euler angles, and the Euler angles into the quaternion

\[(Оё, П•) \mapsto (П•, Оё, 0) \mapsto рқҗҗ = \exp\left(\frac{П•}{2} рқҗӨ\right) \exp\left(\frac{Оё}{2} рқҗЈ\right).\]

It is straightforward to see that we can write $f$ as a function of $рқҗҗ$ as

\[f(рқҗҗ) = \left\langle рқҗҗ\, рқҗӨ\, рқҗҗ^{-1} \right\rangle_{рқҗЈ},\]

where the angle brackets and subscript indicate that we are taking the $рқҗЈ$ component. That is, $f$ is the $y$ component of the vector $рқҗі$ rotated by $рқҗҗ$.

Now, we imagine rotating the field by an angle $Оұ$ in the positive sense about the $z$ axis. Visualizing the situation, we can see that the rotated field should be represented by

\[f'(Оё, П•) = \sin Оё \sin(П• - Оұ).\]

For example, the rotated field evaluated at the point $(Оё, П•) = (ПҖ/2, 0)$ along the positive $x$ axis should correspond to the original field evaluated at the point $(Оё, П•) = (ПҖ/2, -Оұ)$. This rotation is generated by $рқ”Ө = Оұ рқҗӨ / 2$, which allows us to immediately calculate

\[\begin{aligned} f(e^рқ”Ө рқҗҗ) &= \sin Оё \sin(П• + Оұ) &&& f(рқҗҗ e^рқ”Ө) &= \sin Оё \sin П• \\ f(e^{-рқ”Ө} рқҗҗ) &= \sin Оё \sin(П• - Оұ) &&& f(рқҗҗ e^{-рқ”Ө}) &= \sin Оё \sin П•. \end{aligned}\]

Thus, we see that left-multiplication by $e^{-рқ”Ө}$ corresponds to rotation of the field while leaving the coordinates fixed; left-multiplication by $e^рқ”Ө$ corresponds to rotation of the coordinates while leaving the field fixed; and right-multiplication by either doesn't affect this function at all.

Of course, right-multiplication using other choices for $рқ”Ө$ could certainly have some effect on this function, and this choice of $рқ”Ө$ could have an effect on other functions. Note that right-multiplication can also be interpreted as left-multiplication, where the generator itself is rotated by the argument to the function. That is,

\[\begin{aligned} f(рқҗҗ e^рқ”Ө) &= f(рқҗҗ e^{рқ”Ө} рқҗҗ^{-1} рқҗҗ) = f(e^{рқ”Ө'} рқҗҗ) \\ f(рқҗҗ e^{-рқ”Ө}) &= f(рқҗҗ e^{-рқ”Ө} рқҗҗ^{-1} рқҗҗ) = f(e^{-рқ”Ө'} рқҗҗ), \end{aligned}\]

where $рқ”Ө' = рқҗҗ рқ”Ө рқҗҗ^{-1}$. In this example, $рқ”Ө'$ generates a rotation by an angle $Оұ$ about the point in question, which leaves that point fixed, and since this is a scalar function it has no effect on the value. Of course, we will see below that changing by a phase proportional to $Оұ$ is the defining feature of a spin-weighted function.

Differential rotations

We now define a pair of operators that differentiate a function with respect to infinitesimal rotations we apply to the functions themselves:

\[\begin{aligned} L_{рқ”Ө} f(рқҗҗ) &= \lambda \left. \frac{\partial} {\partial Оё} f \left( e^{-Оё рқ”Ө / 2} рқҗҗ \right) \right|_{Оё=0}, \\ R_{рқ”Ө} f(рқҗҗ) &= \rho \left. \frac{\partial} {\partial Оё} f \left( рқҗҗ e^{-Оё рқ”Ө / 2} \right) \right|_{Оё=0}. \end{aligned}\]

Here, we have introduced the constants $\lambda$ and $\rho$ because we will actually be able to derive their values вҖ” up to signs вҖ” based on the requirement that raising and lowering operators exist for each. Finally, we will choose the signs based on demands that these operators correspond as naturally as possible to the standard canonical angular-momentum operators.

Note that when composing operators, it is critical to keep track of the order of operations, which may look slightly unnatural:

\[\begin{aligned} L_рқ”Ө L_рқ”Ҙ f(рқҗҗ) % &= \left. \lambda \frac{\partial} {\partial Оі} f'\left(e^{-Оі рқ”Ө / 2} рқҗҗ \right) \right|_{Оі=0}, \\ &= \left. \lambda^2 \frac{\partial} {\partial Оі} \frac{\partial} {\partial \eta} f\left(e^{-\eta рқ”Ҙ / 2} e^{-Оі рқ”Ө / 2} рқҗҗ \right) \right|_{Оі=\eta=0}, \\ R_рқ”Ө R_рқ”Ҙ f(рқҗҗ) % &= \rho \left. \frac{\partial} {\partial Оі} f' \left( рқҗҗ e^{-Оі рқ”Ө / 2} \right) \right|_{Оі=0} \\ &= \left. \rho^2 \frac{\partial} {\partial Оі} \frac{\partial} {\partial \eta} f\left( рқҗҗ e^{-Оі рқ”Ө / 2} e^{-\eta рқ”Ҙ / 2} \right) \right|_{Оі=\eta=0}. \end{aligned}\]

We can prove the first of these, for example, by defining $f'(рқҗҗ) = L_рқ”Ҙ f(рқҗҗ)$, then applying the definition of $L_рқ”Ө$ to $f'(рқҗҗ)$, and finally substituting the definition of $f'$ back in. If we failed to use the correct order of operations, we would get sign errors when trying to evaluate the commutators.

These operators have some nice properties. For any scalar $s$, we have

\[\begin{aligned} L_{s рқ”Ө} &= s L_{рқ”Ө}, \\ R_{s рқ”Ө} &= s R_{рқ”Ө}. \end{aligned}\]

Given any basis $рқҗһ_n$ for the quaternions, we can use the multivariable chain rule to expand the operators in terms of components:

\[\begin{aligned} L_{рқ”Ө} &= \sum_n g_n\, L_{рқҗһ_n}, \\ R_{рқ”Ө} &= \sum_n g_n\, R_{рқҗһ_n}. \end{aligned}\]

This implies that vector addition holds more generally:

\[\begin{aligned} L_{рқ”Ө + рқ”Ҙ} &= L_{рқ”Ө} + L_{рқ”Ҙ} \\ R_{рқ”Ө + рқ”Ҙ} &= R_{рқ”Ө} + R_{рқ”Ҙ}. \end{aligned}\]

Moreover, we can show that these operators form a Lie algebra with the commutator as the Lie bracket. That is, we have

\[\begin{aligned} [L_{рқ”Ө}, L_{рқ”Ҙ}] &= \frac{\lambda}{2} L_{[рқ”Ө, рқ”Ҙ]}, \\ [R_{рқ”Ө}, R_{рқ”Ҙ}] &= -\frac{\rho}{2} R_{[рқ”Ө, рқ”Ҙ]}, \\ [L_{рқ”Ө}, R_{рқ”Ҙ}] &= 0. \end{aligned}\]

Conventionally, we single out the $рқҗі$ axis вҖ” or equivalently the generator $рқҗӨ = рқҗІрқҗұ$ вҖ” as a sort of fiducial axis, and $L_z = L_рқҗӨ$ and $R_z = R_рқҗӨ$ as the fiducial operators. Then, by definition, their raising operators $L_+$ and $R_+$ and lowering operators $L_-$ and $R_-$ satisfy

\[\begin{aligned} [L_z, L_\pm] &= \pm L_\pm, \\ [R_z, R_\pm] &= \pm R_\pm. \end{aligned}\]

Assuming that the raising and lowering operators can be written as linear combinations of the basis operators, these equations imply that they have no component proportional $L_рқҗі$, and that both of the remaining components must be nonzero. This actually allows us to deduce that $\lambda^2 = \rho^2 = -1$. This, in turn, allows us to deduce the values of the raising and lowering operators up to an overall factor. Conventionally the factor is chosen so that

\[\begin{aligned} L_\pm &= L_рқҗұ \pm i L_рқҗІ, \\ R_\pm &= R_рқҗұ \pm i R_рқҗІ. \end{aligned}\]

Laplacians

Bander and Itzykson [17] show that Wigner's D matrices (extended to the full space of quaternions with arbitrary norm) are harmonic with respect to the Laplacian of the full 4-D space. We also know that

\[\Delta_{рқ•Ҡ^{n-1}} f(x) = \Delta_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x/|x|),\]

and

\[\Delta_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x) = \frac{1}{r^{n-1}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^{n-1} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \right) + \frac{1}{r^2} \Delta_{рқ•Ҡ^{n-1}} f.\]

These imply that the restriction to the space of unit quaternions is not harmonic with respect to the Laplacian on the 3-sphere, but is an eigenfunction with eigenvalue $-в„“(в„“+2)$.

\[\frac{1}{r^{n-1}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^{n-1} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \right) = \frac{1}{r^{n-1}} \left( r^{n-1} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \right) + \frac{1}{r^{n-1}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^{n-1} \right) \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial r^2} + \frac{n-1}{r^{n-1}} r^{n-2} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial r^2} + \frac{n-1}{r} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\]

\[\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial r^2} + \frac{n-1}{r} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = \frac{f}{r^в„“} \frac{\partial^2 r^в„“}{\partial r^2} + \frac{f}{r^в„“} \frac{n-1}{r} \frac{\partial r^в„“}{\partial r} = в„“(в„“-1) \frac{f}{r^в„“} r^{в„“-2} + в„“ \frac{f}{r^в„“} \frac{n-1}{r} r^{в„“-1} = в„“(в„“-1) \frac{f}{r^2} + в„“ (n-1) \frac{f}{r^2} = в„“(в„“+n-2) \frac{f}{r^2} \to в„“(в„“+2) \frac{f}{r^2}\]

Note that Lee [18] points out that there is a sign ambiguity in the Laplacian. As I see it, the geometry community skews toward including a negative sign (which means that all eigenvalues are non-negative), while the physics community skews toward excluding it (which means that all eigenvalues are non-positive). It's also easy to prove that on a closed and connected manifold, eigenfunctions with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal, since

\[(\lambda_u - \lambda_v) \int f_u f_v = \int (\lambda_u f_u) f_v - \int f_u (\lambda_v f_v) = \int (\Delta f_u) f_v - \int f_u (\Delta f_v) = 0\]

(the last equality by Green's theorem). Since the eigenvalues are distinct, this can only be true if $\int f_u f_v=0$.

Boyd and Petschek [19] produced an interesting discussion with numerous little insights into the use of special functions on different spaces. In particular, they show why associated Legendre functions are preferred to Chebyshev polynomials for the spherical harmonics. They also mention that since the Laplacian measures curvature, and spherical harmonics of a given degree have the same Laplacian eigenvalue, they all have the same measure of curvature. So, for example, the $в„“ = m$ mode varies most rapidly with longitude but not at all with latitude, while the $в„“ = 0$ mode varies just as rapidly with latitude but not at all with longitude.

Vasil et al. [20] use spin-weighted spherical harmonics to do tensor calculus in the 3-ball, and have a lot formulas for derivatives, as a result.

\[|Оұ\rangle_R = \mathscr{D}(R) |Оұ\rangle,\]

$|Оұ\rangle_R$ and $|Оұ\rangle$ stand for the kets of the rotated and original system, respectively.

If the field is represented as a function $f(рқҗ‘)$, then rotating the field by $e^{Пө рқҗ®/2}$ is equivalent to rotating the argument of the function by $e^{-Пө рқҗ®/2}$:

    • Has three parts, as given by Wikipedia:
      1. "The matrix coefficients of irreducible representations of $G$ are dense in the space $C(G)$ of continuous complex-valued functions on $G$, and thus also in the space $L^2(G)$ of square-integrable functions."
      2. Unitary representations of $G$ are completely reducible.
      3. "The regular representation of $G$ on $L^2(G)$ decomposes as the direct sum of all irreducible unitary representations. Moreover, the matrix coefficients of the irreducible unitary representations form an orthonormal basis of $L^2(G)$."

Recursion relations

Gumerov and Duraiswami (2001) derive their recursion relations by differentiating solutions of the Helmholtz equation $\nabla^2 ПҲ + k^2 ПҲ = 0$ as $\tfrac{1}{k} \nabla ПҲ$. More precisely, they differentiate both sides of the equation relating one solution to its rotated form вҖ” which naturally involves Wigner's $рқ”Ү$ matrix. Using orthogonal basis functions for the solution, this allows them to equate terms on the two sides proportional to a given basis function, which leaves them with expressions involving sums of only the $рқ”Ү$ matrices and some coefficients depending on the indices of the basis functions (and hence of $рқ”Ү$) on both sides of the equation. Since $\nabla$ is a 3-vector operator, this gives them three relations.

This, of course, is happening in 3-D space, since $ПҲ$ is a function of location in the Helmholtz equation. It seems likely to me, however, that we could use the 4-D (quaternionic) version of the functions. Note that G&D use $\partial_z$ and $\partial_x \pm i \partial_y$ as their operators to differentiate the functions вҖ” that is, the derivatives are with respect to Cartesian coordinates, which may be more similar to the right-derivative defined above. However, I don't know that we'll necessarily be able to achieve the same results with just angular-momentum operators, since their operators do involve moving off of the sphere. Maybe we'd need to move off of the sphere in 4-D space to get comparable results. Or maybe just use something like $рқҗ« вҲ§ L$, which should also have 3 degrees of freedom.
